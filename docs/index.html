what factors impact maternal health in the US?

In the 21st century, a time of unprecedented medical advancements, the United States is grappling with a dire reality: its maternal morbidity rate ranks among the highest in the developed world. In 2018, the U.S. stood 55th globally, with a staggering 17.4 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births. [1] The numbers are growing more troubling each year — 1,205 women died of maternal causes in 2021, up from 861 in 2020 and 754 in 2019. [2]

Experts believe that 84% of these deaths could have been prevented. [3]

What personal health metrics and social determinants impact maternal morbidity in the US? By examining county-level data from the CDC's Births Data Summary [4] and the American Community Survey [5], we’ll visualize the critical factors influencing maternal health, including maternal age, pre-pregnancy BMI, prenatal care, clinic access, poverty, income, race, and education.

Advanced maternal age, commonly defined as 35 years and older, brings a set of heightened risks to pregnancy. This includes an increased likelihood of chromosomal abnormalities like Down syndrome. Additionally, older mothers face higher rates of gestational diabetes and hypertensive disorders, both of which pose significant threats to maternal and fetal health. Conversely, teenage pregnancies also come with their own set of challenges, such as a greater chance of preterm birth and low birth weight. [6],[7]

Prenatal visits are medical check-ups that monitor the progress of the pregnancy and ensure the health of both the mother and the developing fetus. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends a schedule of regular prenatal visits throughout pregnancy, with increased frequency in the later stages. These visits are crucial for monitoring the baby's growth, detecting and managing any potential complications, providing essential prenatal education, and offering support to the expecting mother. Insufficient prenatal care may lead to delayed detection and management of pregnancy-related complications. [8]

Unfortunately, an individual's race or ethnicity is a social determinant of health in the United States. Particularly, racism as a barrier to health equity can cause disparities in healthcare access and outcomes across racial and ethnic groups. [11] To truly grasp the scope of maternal morbidity in this country, it's essential to examine data on race and ethnicity.

The first model predicts that the college educated population of a county is directly proportional to that county’s average age of mothers at childbirth. Interestingly, the second model predicts that as median income of a county increases, the maternal morbidity rate of that county also increases. However, the second model was only able to achieve a R2 value of 0.0014. Training many different prediction models with various metrics as input data (including multiple metrics, instead of just one as above) did not lead to better results, suggesting that maternal morbidity is influenced by complex and multifaceted factors that are difficult to capture through straightforward modeling.

The challenge of understanding and addressing maternal morbidity in the United States is far from straightforward. While certain patterns emerge, such as the relationship between education levels, income, and maternal health, these trends reveal the complexity of the issue rather than provide clear answers.

These visualizations highlight the intricate interplay of social, economic, and healthcare factors that define maternal health outcomes. The path forward demands not only more sophisticated data analysis but also a commitment to systemic change—one that ensures all women, regardless of their background or where they live, can survive childbirth in the 21st century.

methodology

The two primary datasets used in this project were the CDC’s Births Data Summary and the United States Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS). These two datasets each contain data collected at the county level, and can be combined through the unique FIPS code of each county. FIPS codes are used in the U.S. to link geographic areas at the state and county level. [12] Throughout join operations across multiple tables in the Birth's Data Summary dataset, and join operations between the Birth's Data Summary dataset and the ACS dataset, the county FIPS code is used as the foreign key to accurately link the data.

These datasets contain information across multiple years. I chose to examine data from the year 2018, as it was the most recent year recorded in the Births Data Summary dataset. I paired this data with the "5 Year Sample" 2017 data from the ACS dataset, based on the Census Bureau's recommendation that 5-year samples are the most reliable.

While extensive, the Births Data Summary has some limitations. It does not include all counties, and counties included vary based on state. Some states, like California, Texas, and Florida have a high number of counties in the dataset, while other counties such as North Dakota, Montana, and Nebraska only have one or two counties represented. Additionally, the counties appear to center around urban areas. In addition to counties missing from the dataset, the counties included within the Births Data Summary dataset are missing data for some areas. For example, there are 626 total counties, but only 580 of those counties have recorded statistics on maternal deaths.

To augment the concrete data in these two datasets, many metrics were manually calculated and some data was manually retrieved.

I computed the maternal morbidity rate for each county as a morbidity percent, defined as the number of births resulting in a maternal death divided by the total number of births in that county, based on the Births Data Summary dataset. Poverty rate was calculated as the percentage of individuals in a county recorded as living at or below the poverty line, based on data from ACS. Racial demographic population percentages and college educated population percentage were also calculated from the county-level data in ACS.

Classification of counties as urban or rural comes from a separate dataset, the 2020 United States Census. I also manually gathered data from Google Earth to create the distances in Figure 2, with distance to clinics represented as the ratio of the total number of OB-GYN clinics and hospitals located within the county to the total area of the county in square miles.

Lastly, I created various linear regression models for predicting the average age of mothers and maternal morbidity rate for a county. Notably, while the article allows users to interact with a maternal morbidity prediction model trained on median income of counties, I tried out 10-15 other models with various training features that had similarly lackluster performances when tested.
